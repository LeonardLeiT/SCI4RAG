# Size Dependence of Grain Boundary Migration in Metals under Mechanical Loading

Xin Zhou,1,2 Xiuyan Li,1,* and K. Lu1,†

1 Shenyang National Laboratory for Materials Science, Institute of Metal Research, Chinese Academy of Sciences,

72 Wenhua Road, Shenyang 110016, China

2 School of Materials Science and Engineering, University of Science and Technology of China, Hefei 230026, China

(Received 29 November 2018; revised manuscript received 23 January 2019; published 28 March 2019)

The greatly increased grain boundary (GB) mobility in nanograined metals under mechanical loading is distinguished from that in their coarse-grained counterparts. The feature leads to softening of nanograined materials and deviation of strength from the classical Hall-Petch relationship. In this Letter, grain size dependences of GB migration in nanograined Ag, Cu, and Ni under tension were investigated quantitatively in a wide size range. As grain size decreases from submicron, GB migration intensifies and then diminishes below a critical grain size. The GB migration peaks at about 80, 75, and 38 nm in Ag, Cu, and Ni, respectively. The suppression of GB migration below a critical size can be attributed to GB relaxation during sample processing or by postthermal annealing. With relaxed GBs the governing deformation mechanism of nanograins shifts from GB migration to formation of through-grain twins or stacking faults. GB relaxation, analogous to GB segregation, offers a novel approach to stabilizing nanograined materials under mechanical loading.

DOI: 10.1103/PhysRevLett.122.126101

The properties of polycrystalline solids are influenced by grain boundaries (GBs), and hence are grain size dependent. The size dependences of various physical and mechanical properties become more pronounced as grains are refined into the nanometer scale due to the substantially increased density of GBs [1,2]. Additionally, experimental and simulation studies revealed that the structures and behaviors of GBs themselves are also size dependent on the nanometer scale [3–5]. It offers an alternative dimension to tailor material properties on the nanoscale, drawing increasing interest in solid state physics and materials science in the past decades [6–10].

In classical models of mechanical properties of solids, GBs are often treated as “static” geometrical barriers to dislocation motion. Based on this, a well-known grain size dependence of strength is derived, predicting increased strength with decreased grain size, namely, the Hall-Petch relationship [11,12]. However, deviating from this relationship, some nanograined metals exhibit softening with decreasing grain size [1,13,14]. One of the fundamental reasons is that GBs move in response to mechanical loading with concomitant coarsening of nanograins, especially for pure metals, as evidenced by experiments and by atomistic simulations in a number of nanograined metals and alloys under various loading conditions such as tension [15–17], compression [18], and indentation [19,20], even at cryogenic temperatures. The mechanically induced GB migration not only deteriorates properties of nanograined materials but also hinders their processing by plastic deformation.

The intrinsic mechanism of the mechanically driven GB migration is still in debate. Several mechanisms have been proposed [21–27] including the stress-coupling model [25,26] and the dislocation model [27]. Molecular dynamics simulations showed that under mechanical loading GBs migrate through obvious atomic shuffling in the boundary region and emission of dislocations [28], suggesting the process is dependent of GB structure and composition. This is supported by a recent experimental observation that with segregation of Mo at GBs of Ni nanograins of a few nanometers in size [29], GB mobility is effectively suppressed under mechanical loading [29].

Recently, an abnormal grain size dependence of thermal stability was identified in nanograined pure Cu and Ni samples. Thermal GB stability in Cu drops with grain size decreasing from submicrometers to about $7 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ . Below $7 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ , GB stability increases as grain size decreases. The reversed thermal stability below a critical size originates from an autonomous GB relaxation accompanied by a GB energy drop during plastic straining of nanograined metals [30]. It is, therefore, crucial to exploit the grain size dependence of GB migration and the effect of GB relaxation under mechanical loading in nanograined metals.

In this study gradient nanograined (GNG) samples were used as in the previous study on thermal stability [30]. With the unique strain delocalization mechanism, gradient nanograined structures enable very large tensile strains without failure in the nanograined layer [15,31] that offers an opportunity for measuring the mechanical GB mobility in a wide grain size spectrum.

For producing GNG structures, coarse-grained Cu dogbone-shaped tensile specimens with a purity of $9 9 . 9 7 \%$ were processed using a surface mechanical grinding treatment (SMGT) at cryogenic temperatures (processing details in Refs. [30,32]). After the treatment, a GNG surface layer of the gauge section is generated while the sample core is coarse grained: average transversal grain sizes increase from $4 2 \pm 1 1 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ (with an aspect ratio of 1.7) in the topmost surface to $2 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ at a depth of $7 0 \ \mu \mathrm { { m } }$ . Detailed microstructures were described in Ref. [30]. No change in texture was found from the topmost surface to $4 0 \ \mu \mathrm { { m } }$ depth, and processing-induced contaminations (such as carbon, oxygen, and nitrogen) were not detected in the surface layer deeper than $0 . 5 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ from the processed surface. Measurement data from the topmost $0 . 5 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ thick layer were removed from our analysis to avoid negligible contamination and possible surface effects.

As revealed previously [30], in the as-prepared SMGT sample (Cu-1) GB relaxation occurred in the top surface layer of roughly $2 0 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ thick in which grains are smaller than about $7 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ . In deeper layers with grains larger than $7 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , no obvious GB relaxation was detected. Therefore, the GB relaxation effect on mechanical GB migration can be examined by simple quasistatic uniaxial tension of the GNG Cu samples at room temperature (Fig. S1 [32]). After tension, the nanograined surface layer deforms uniformly with the coarse-grained core to a uniform elongation of $31 \%$ without any surface cracking or delaminating. In cross sections of the tensioned specimens obvious grain coarsening was observed in the surface layer of $7 0 \ \mu \mathrm { { m } }$ depth (Fig. 1). Deeper than $7 0 \ \mu \mathrm { { m } }$ coarsening is hardly identified. The grain coarsening layer was verified by a microhardness drop (softening), in contrast to hardening in the core with coarse grains, consistent with previous observations [22,36]. Statistical measurements under TEM showed that the average grain size in the topmost layer (in a depth span of $1 { - } 2 \ \mu \mathrm { m } ,$ ) increases from $4 4 \pm 1 0$ to $4 8 \pm 1 3 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ after tension [Figs. 1(c), 1(d), Fig. S2 [32] ]. But in the subsurface layer coarsening is evident, from $1 0 6 \pm 3 2$ to $1 7 0 \pm 4 1 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ at a depth of $3 0 \ \mu \mathrm { { m } }$ [Figs. 1(e), 1(f)]. As the tensile strain is identical (0.31) in the entire specimen, one may derive the GB migration at different grain sizes with a given strain in one sample. According to the grain size profiles measured along depth before and after tension [Fig. 1(g)], the change in grain size peaks at a depth of $1 5 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ with a maximum value of $90 \%$ [Fig. 1(i)]. It drops with a decreasing depth below $1 5 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ and becomes negligible at the topmost surface. A similar grain coarsening phenomenon was also observed as the sample was tensioned at 77 K (Fig. S3 [32]) [37].

Another sample (Cu-2) with larger nanograins in the topmost surface was prepared by using the same SMGT process at lower strain rates [32]. Microstructures of the coarsening layer were characterized under TEM. For the initial grains larger than $7 5 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ in Cu, typical submicron-sized grains containing some dislocations with distinct GBs are formed after tension [Fig. 3(c)], without twin or stacking fault. In contrast, in plenty of grains with initial sizes below $7 5 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ [as circled in Fig. 3(a)], throughgrain twins and/or stacking faults were detected [Fig. 3(b)]. Twins and/or stacking faults in other grains (not circled) were seen by tilting the sample. Twin thickness ranges from a few to ten nanometers, much smaller than that in conventionallydeformed Cu ${ \sim } 4 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ [42]), meaning a very high local shear stress applied. Twins exist in some very fine nanograins before tension. But the statistic twin density increases tremendously after tension at room temperature.

In the tensioned Ni sample, coarsening of large grains $\left( > 4 0 \ \mathrm { n m } \right)$ is dominated by GB migration, analogous to that in Cu. For grains smaller than $3 8 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , slightly coarsening was observed after tension but many more stacking faults in almost each nanograin [Figs. 3(d), 3(e)] in comparison with that prior to tension, suggesting formation of stacking faults activated by very high shear stresses dominates plastic

deformation. These evidences point out that suppression of the mechanically induced GB migration in these metals corresponds to a deformation mechanism shift to deformation twinning and/or stacking faults as grains are smaller than a critical size.

Both experimental and computational examples showed that plastic deformation can relax structures of GBs in nanograined materials [43,44]. Emission of partial dislocations from a GB, forming either stacking faults or twins in neighboring grains, was proposed to trigger GB relaxation via GB dissociation processes to lower energy states [45–47]. It provides a general mode of GB relaxation especially in metals with low stacking fault energy like Cu. GB energy can be lowered due to formation of nanometer-scale twins from the GB. Chen et al. [48] observed that atomic diffusion is remarkably retarded and GB electromigration slowed down along the GBs adjacent to the triple point where they meet a twin boundary in Cu.

GBs can also be relaxed by thermal annealing under proper conditions. The as-prepared SMGT nanograined sample (Cu-1) was heated rapidly to $2 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and held for 15 min for inducing GB relaxation of nanograins with sizes around $8 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ in the subsurface layer $1 5 \mathrm { - } 2 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { m }$ deep). After annealing, no obvious change was detected in average grain size (from $8 3 \pm 2 7$ to $8 2 \pm 2 5 ~ \mathrm { n m } ,$ ) and in morphology [Figs. 4(a), 4(b)]. The thermal stability of this layer is much elevated, indicative of a relaxation process of GBs during annealing [46]. The annealed sample (Cu-1T) was tensioned to a uniform strain of 0.31 under the same condition of $\mathrm { C u - 1 }$ . Interestingly, after tension there is no detectable grain size change and the average size remains $8 4 \pm 2 6 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ [Fig. 4(c)], distinct from that of the corresponding layer in sample Cu-1, in which the average size increases to $1 3 8 \pm 3 9 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ after tension. Under close TEM observations, plenty of twins were found in nanograins after tension, as circled in Fig. 4(c). By tilting the samples, twins or stacking faults can be seen in many other grains.

The consistent results in two different sets of experiments indicate that the mechanical GB migration is effectively suppressed by GB relaxation, induced either mechanically or thermally [46,48,49], while the dominant plastic deformation mechanism switches to formation of twins and stacking faults in nanograins. The deformation mechanism change agrees with earlier observations. The mechanical coarsening becomes reluctant in Co-P nanograined alloys when deformation is dominated by twinning or formation of stacking faults, while the coarsening is obvious as deformation is governed by full dislocation activity in a nanograined Ni-Fe sample [47]. With segregation of Mo at GBs, the mechanically induced coarsening of Ni nanograins is suppressed and the deformation mechanism is substituted by generation of stacking faults inside the Ni nanograins [29].

This can be understood by comparing the critical shear stress needed to nucleate a perfect dislocation with that required to initiate Shockley partial dislocations, with an approximation of the source size equal to the grain size $( D )$ . The comparison can be made in terms of the Orowan relation [50] in the classical dislocation theory. Full dislocation multiplication requires that Frank-Read-type sources have a minimum grain size $( D ^ { * } )$ at the yield strength $( \sigma _ { y } )$ which is grain size dependent; i.e., bowing out of full dislocations is inhibited as grain sizes are smaller than $D ^ { * }$ while initiation of partial dislocations is preferred:

$$
D ^ {*} = \frac {\mu b}{\sigma_ {y} \left(D ^ {*}\right) / m}, \tag {1}
$$

where $1 / m$ is the average Schmid factor for polycrystals $( 1 / 3 )$ .

Dislocation bowing out at the most favored slip system with the largest resolved shear stress $( 1 / m = 1 / 2 )$ is inhibited as grains are smaller than $D ^ { * * } { = } \mu b / [ \sigma _ { y } ( D ^ { * * } ) / 2 ]$ . It means for grains smaller than $D ^ { * * }$ , only partials are emitted while nucleation of full dislocations is completely inhibited. Based on the measured yield strength of the nanograined metals [32], the critical sizes were calculated, being

$D ^ { * } = 7 7 , 7 0$ , and $4 3 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , and $D ^ { * * } = 4 0$ , 35, and $2 2 \mathrm { n m }$ for Ag, Cu, and Ni, respectively. $D ^ { * }$ values for $\mathrm { C u }$ and Ni are very close to that reported in Ref. [51]. The calculated $D ^ { * }$ values are approximately in agreement with the measured inflection sizes in the three metals, respectively. And the calculated $D ^ { * * }$ values are also consistent with the measured noncoarsening stable grain sizes $\Delta D = 0$ ).

Although the classical dislocation model used here is very simplified, the agreement between the calculated results and the experimental observations are surprisingly satisfactory. The minor differences between them may be attributed to the slight texture in the nanograins, inaccuracy of the input parameters (especially the grain size dependence of stress), and measurement errors in grain sizes. Hence, the suppression of the mechanically induced GB migration with GB relaxation below a critical size can be reasonably attributed to inhibition of full dislocations and nucleation of partials to form twins or stacking faults in nanograins. The observed GB relaxation effect is fundamentally related to the interface phenomenon in thin films and nanolaminates widely presented in electronic devices. Analogous to the GB relaxation in pure metals, formation of a low-energy interface in bilayer heterostructures has been observed in the literatures as their sizes are reduced down to the nanometer scale, forming more stable interfaces, as observed in Al=Pb [52] and $\mathrm { { C u / N b } }$ [53] nanolaminates.

An important implication of this finding is that very fine nanograins even in pure metals can be mechanically stable as long as GBs are relaxed mechanically or thermally. Thus, structures of metals could be further refined and stabilized below the critical sizes with plastic deformation. GB relaxation offers a novel strategy stabilizing nanograined metals under mechanical stimuli.
