# Materials and Methods

# 1. Sample preparation

Polycrystalline pure Cu samples with extremely fine grains were prepared using a two-step cryogenic plastic deformation process. Cu bars with a purity of $9 9 . 9 7 \ \%$ were annealed at $4 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $^ { 2 \mathrm { h } }$ to form fully recrystallized structures with coarse grains. In the first step, the coarse-grained Cu bar was processed using a surface mechanical grinding treatment (SMGT (35)) in liquid nitrogen with processing parameters listed in Table S1. For each sample, the SMGT process was repeated several times in order to achieve a large accumulative plastic strain in the surface layer (an estimated strain larger than 100). The strain rates in the top surface layer was estimated to be $1 0 ^ { 3 } \mathrm { s ^ { - 1 } }$ . Due to formation of a gradient nanograined surface layer that is effective in suppressing strain localization, plastic deformation is rather uniform in the sample surface with a surface roughness Ra≈ $0 . 3 \mu \mathrm { m }$ . No crack and contamination were identified in the surface layer of the SMGT sample. In the top surface layer of $2 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ thick of the Cu rod, highly stable nanograins are formed. This layer is then removed from the rod and processed further in the second step.

In the second step, the foil sample cut from the SMGT Cu rod was electropolished in an electrolyte (phosphoric acid: alcohol: deionized water=1:1:2) for removing possible contamination. Then the foil was deformed by using a high pressure torsion (HPT) facility on a rectangular flat die with a processing pressure up to $1 0 \mathrm { G P a }$ . The number of the revolving cycles is 5 with a rate of about $3 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ . The samples and dies were immersed in liquid nitrogen during the entire HPT deformation process. The schematic of the HPT processing is displayed in Fig. S1. After the treatment, grains were refined into the nanometer scale in the as-HPT sample. The sample contains extremely-fine grained (below $1 0 \mathrm { n m }$ ) domains of several to ten microns in size embedded in regions with larger grains (averagely about $2 5 \mathrm { n m }$ in size).

# 2. Structure characterization

Structural characterization of the as-prepared samples and the annealed samples were performed on a scanning electron microscope (SEM, FEI Verios 460) and transmission electron microscopes (TEM, FEI Talos F200X, FEI Titan ChemisSTEM, FEI Titan Themis), respectively. The sample preparation for SEM and TEM observations follows the ordinary route of cutting, mechanical polishing, and final electro-chemical polishing or thinning. The final finishing was accomplished by electro-chemical method using an electrolyte consisting of $2 5 \%$ alcohol, $2 5 \%$ phosphorus acid and $5 0 \%$ de-ionized water at $0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ .

# 3. Hardness measurement

For the nanograined samples with average grain sizes of $2 5 \mathrm { n m }$ and $1 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , nanohardness tests were performed on a Hysitron Tribo-indenter with the measurement conditions of a preset load of $3 0 0 0 \mu \mathrm { N } _ { \mathrm { \Omega } }$ , a loading and unloading rate of $6 0 0 \mu \mathrm { N } / \mathrm { s }$ , and a holding time of 2 seconds. Typical load-displacement curves measured from extremely fine grains regions are displayed Fig. S2. For the as-SMGT samples with grain sizes larger than $4 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , Vickers hardness measurements were conducted on a Qness $\mathrm { Q 1 0 A + }$ automatic hardness tester with a load of $2 5 \mathrm { g }$ and a holding time of 10 s. Meanwhile, nanohardness of grains with sizes range from $4 0 \mathrm { n m }$ to $2 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ are also measured for

comparison, as seen in Fig. S2. The nanohardness values depicted in Fig. 3 were averaged with at least 10 indentations.

# 4. Thermal stability measurement

Thermal stability of the nanograined Cu samples was characterized by means of isothermal annealing. The samples were sealed in vacuum tubes together with small pieces of pure titanium for preventing oxidation during annealing. The sealed samples were annealed in a tube furnace with protecting Ar atmosphere and a temperature control accuracy of $\pm 2 \mathrm { \textrm { K } }$ .

The as-prepared samples were heated rapidly (about $1 0 0 \mathrm { K / m i n } ,$ ) to different preset temperatures, holding for 15 min and cooling down for structure characterization. The annealing temperatures in present work are: 523, 573, 673, 773, 873, 973, 1073, 1173, 1223, 1273, 1318, and 1348 K, respectively. Figure S3 showed the typical bright field TEM images of nanograins with an initial grain size of about $1 0 \mathrm { n m }$ in the as-prepared sample and as-annealed (at 1173 K, 1273 K, 1348 K) samples, as well as their corresponding statistical grain size distributions.

# 5. Construction of 3D CTB networks

The original solution of the "Kelvin problem" consists only two truncated-octahedra (tetrakaidecahedra) of equal size, the centers of which (grains K1 and K2, Fig. 4A) form a body centered cubic (BCC) array filling the space (36). Depending on the lattice orientations of K1 and K2, four pairs of GBs can be formed, with GB planes perpendicular to each {111} orientations of the supercell. However, no GB can be formed in any {001} direction in such a way.

An extended Kelvin construction was used to render all surfaces of individual truncated-octahedra into grain boundaries, which involves sixteen equal-sized tetrakaidecahedra packing into four layers (Fig. S4), where eight of them are packed first to fill up the upper-half of the box and then, duplicated and shifted by half the box length in X, Y and Z directions, respectively, to fill up the lower-half of the box.

The constructed GB network depends on lattice orientations of individual grains and the way to combine them. Taken polyhedron O1 with cubic orientations of a FCC lattice as the center cell (Fig. S4), the lattice orientations of its eight nearest neighboring polyhedra, i.e. A1, B1, C1, D1 (upper panel) and A2, B2, C2, D2 (lower panel), must be chosen to be in a mirror-reflected pattern such that in each ${ < } 1 1 1 { > }$ orientation a Σ3-type CTB can be formed. According to this rule, ${ < } 2 1 1 >$ -type lattice orientations can be specified to the NN-polyhedra (29, 37).

With respect to O1, the assignment of lattice orientations to its second nearest neighboring (2nd-NN) polyhedra (i.e. I1, E1 and F1 sitting at the same layer) is not strongly limited. Here, the Σ9-type GBs are considered for all 2nd-NN polyhedral. In particular, their orientations are chosen such that in each ${ \mathrm { < } } 0 0 1 { \mathrm { > } }$ orientation (i.e. X, Y and Z directions of the supercell) of O1 or O2, a symmetric tilt Σ9 GB is formed. In addition, it is guaranteed that GBs between any pairs of O1 or O2’s neighboring polyhedra are also Σ9-type GBs. Detailed lattice orientations of individual grains within the extended Kelvin box were given in Table S2.

In the as-constructed specimen, the number of grains (N), grain faces (F), grain edges (E), and grain corners (C), equals to 16, 112, 192 and 96, respectively. They obey

the Euler’s rules $\mathrm { ( N + E = F + C }$ , $\mathrm { N + C = F }$ and $\mathrm { E } { = } 2 \mathrm { C }$ ) with a network connectivity $\mathrm { F } { \cdot } \mathrm { N } { + } 1$ or $\mathrm { E - C + 1 }$ ) of 97 according to the network law (28). Grain edges are triple lines (TLs) and each TL is formed by three grains. Grain corners or nodes of the GB-network are quadrupolar points (QPs), where four TLs meet at each QP, as in Fig. S4A.

There is no limit on the size of the polyhedra as long as the enclosed GB portion can be well-defined down to the atomic scale. A sample of Cu is constructed with an elemental grain size (the height of a truncated-octahedron in ${ \mathrm { < 0 1 0 > } }$ directions), $\scriptstyle \mathrm { D = } 9 \mathrm { a _ { 0 } }$ or $3 . 2 7 \mathrm { n m }$ (a0, the lattice parameter), comparable to those in experiments. The supercell contains 22918 atoms (Fig. S4B). As specified, a space-filling 3D CTB network is constructed as a sub-GB network. If looking outward within O1 or O2, one found that in each ${ < } 1 1 1 { > }$ direction, only CTBs show up periodically from one cell to the next (Fig. S4B). That is, four separate sets of intertwined channels are formed. This topological feature serves to reveal the geometry of polycrystals with high density of CTBs behind random and seemingly-complicated manifold.

# 6. MD simulations and visualizations

The thermal stability is assessed via thermal relaxations in NPT-MD simulations. In particular, we follow a heating procedure to approach a target temperature at a heating rate of $8 ~ \mathrm { K / n s }$ during which the temperature is elevated at an interval of 1 K. Freezing is carried out as a reverse procedure.

In uniaxial tensile loading test, a tension strain is applied homogeneously at a constant rate in X, Y or Z direction of the MD supercell during which the system volume is kept constant. With our MD sample, tensile loading in X, Y or Z-direction, respectively, are found to produce similar results. Three sets of strain rate $( 1 0 ^ { 5 } / \mathrm { s } , 1 0 ^ { 6 } / \mathrm { s }$ and $1 0 ^ { 7 } / \mathrm { s } \mathrm { \dot { \Omega } }$ ) were chosen to examine rate effects.

In both the heating and loading procedures, atomic positions and velocities are integrated and updated each time-step (2.5 fs) on Nose-Hoover canonical style to maintain a constant temperature. The interatomic potential is based on the semi-empirical model developed with the embedded atom method (EAM), a reliable classical atomistic model for simulations of crystal defects in Cu (30). With common neighbor analyses (CNA), atoms at stacking faults, twin boundaries and other defects can be separated from those at FCC lattice sites. Grain boundaries can be visualized as a portion of continues interface using adequate means such as dislocation analyses (DXA) in combination with planar defect meshing technique implemented in atomic analyses and visualization software called Ovito (38, 39). All simulations were carried out using the MD package LAMMPS (40).

# Supplementary Text

# 1. Transformation from Kelvin to Schwarz crystal

The nanograins represented by extended Kelvin polyhedra undergoes significant structural relaxations upon heating, as shown in Fig. S5. For $\mathrm { T } < 6 4 0 \mathrm { K }$ , relaxations of GB atoms occur strongly, in particular, those at corners (QPs) and edges (triple junction lines of GBs) of grains. GB reactions (29) may occur at low temperatures $( \mathrm { T } < 4 0 0 \ : \mathrm { K } )$ or even merely via energy minimizations at zero temperature. Comparing to the initial structure, enhanced geometry complications can be observed. At the temperatures above

400 K, GB atoms became more mobile and diffusive. Fractions of GB atoms and CTB atoms fluctuate strongly with rising the temperatures to $\sim 0 . 5 \ T _ { m }$ .

Enormous atomic events occur during $6 4 0 \mathrm { K } { < } \mathrm { T } { < } 7 3 0 \mathrm { K }$ . GB atoms became highly mobile and diffusive. Migrations of both GBs and CTBs are observed. Grains associated with the intertwined channels (O1 and O2) expanded at the expense of other grains and developed into a pair of twinned tetrahedra. Twinning is accomplished via Shockley partials activated mainly at the nodes of the GB network. Most grains were coalesced, through which the original GB-network disappeared but the CTB-network remains. Transformation of GBs into the Schwarz D-surface form (32) is observed at about 750 K. The transition temperature may vary depending on the heating rates, the size of Kelvin grains, and other factors such as the orientations of the second neighboring grains (those not bounded completely by CTBs). For example, for very tiny Kelvin grains (size below $4 \mathrm { n m } ,$ ), higher heating above 8 K/ns facilitates the transformation within a narrow temperature window from $6 0 0 \mathrm { K }$ to 750 K. For larger Kelvin grains (size about 6 nm), the transition may occur at lower heating rates, but also at a higher transformation temperature close to the melting point. For even larger grains (size about $1 0 \mathrm { n m }$ ), the transition became sluggish and incomplete.

The areas of GB and CTB in the Schwarz D-structure can be estimated more precisely in terms of the averaged number density per unit area of grain boundary, which is $0 . 1 7 4 \mathrm { ~ } / \mathring { \mathrm { A } } ^ { 2 }$ for CTB atoms $\left( \rho _ { \mathrm { C T B } } \right)$ and $0 . 4 1 { \sim } 0 . 4 \dot { 3 } / \dot { \mathrm { A } } ^ { 2 }$ for GB atoms (ρGB) quantified via separate MD calculations. Taken that GB atoms and CTB atoms are $1 6 \%$ and $1 0 \%$ respectively in the Schwarz crystal, the area of Schwarz D-surface (ASD) is about 8738 $\mathring { \mathbf { A } } 2$ , almost $6 7 \%$ of the total area covered by the CTBs (ACTB) of the twinned tetrahedra (13029 $\mathring { \mathrm { A } } ^ { 2 }$ ). For our MD cubic cell with a size $\mathrm { L } = 6 5 . 3 \mathrm { 8 } \mathring \mathrm { A }$ , the ASD is nearly 2.04 times the cubic area of the box $( \mathbf { L } ^ { 2 } )$ , agrees with Mackay (33) and Schoen (41) who predicted numerically that ASD is about $1 . 9 2 \sim 2 . 4 2 \mathrm { L } ^ { 2 }$ .

Also noted that for our initial sample containing only regular Kelvin tetrakaidecahedra (unrelaxed), the total surface area shared by the 16 grains in the cubic box (the same size, $6 5 . 3 8 \mathrm { \AA }$ is $2 8 6 2 3 \mathrm { ~ \AA ~ } ^ { 2 }$ , among which $5 5 5 \Dot { 3 } \ \mathring { \mathrm { A } } ^ { 2 }$ is occupied by CTBs. One can see that, after relaxation and the transformation, the total GB area (23070 Å2) has been decreased by $6 2 \%$ . The area of CTB, on the other hand, has been more than doubled (from 5553 $\bar { \mathrm { \AA } } ^ { 2 }$ to $1 3 0 2 9 \mathring { \mathbf { A } } ^ { 2 \cdot }$ ), mostly due to GB reactions before the transformation. The average energy of the GBs assembling the Schwarz D-surface is estimated to be $5 2 \sim 5 5 \mathrm { m e V } / \mathring { \mathrm { A } } ^ { 2 }$ or $8 3 0 \sim 8 8 0 \mathrm { m J } / \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 }$ at $0 \mathrm { \ K }$ , nearly 40 times of the CTB energy $\mathrm { 1 . 3 7 5 ~ m e V / \mathring { A } ^ { 2 } }$ or $2 2 \mathrm { m J } / \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 }$ ).

As one set of fundamental triply periodic minimal surfaces (a surface of everywhere vanishing mean curvature) in the mathematical theory, the configuration of Schwarz Dsurface is symmetrically identical to a diamond lattice, which gives the surface its name, D (32, 41). As such, the nanostructure where GBs can be characterized as the D-surface, or, any other forms of minimal surfaces in general (41), can be termed as Schwarz crystal.

# 2. Thermal stability of Schwarz crystal

The Schwarz crystal demonstrates enhanced thermal stability. As revealed in Fig. S6 A, the nanostructure can survive up to 1322 K, a temperature close to the thermal equilibrium melting point of Cu, $T _ { m } = 1 3 5 6 ~ \mathrm { K }$ . Melting occurs as liquid phase is initiated

at GBs. Neither GB migration nor twinning was observed prior to melting. Without locking effects of CTB network, GBs can hardly achieve such a superior thermal stability. This can be shown by examining the mobility of individual GBs, either symmetric or asymmetric, flat or curved. A few examples are given in Fig. S6 B-D.

Flat GBs are examined using bicrystal supercells built with lattice orientations specified in Table S2. For example, placing a grain B on top of a grain C will generate a pair of symmetric Σ9 (22̅1̅) tilt GBs in a MD supercell; while placing B on top of D, on the other hand, will generate a pair of asymmetric Σ9 (22̅1̅) GBs of both tilt and twist character (42). It is found that such flat GBs are quite mobile at temperatures below half of the melting point (Fig. S6 B-C). The CTB is found to be most stable, it remains immobile and seems capable to sustain a temperature beyond the melting point, underlying that atoms at CTB is as stable as those in lattice sites (43). Note that the GB mobility is also found depending on the size of the MD supercell, indicating that developing significant curvatures via thermal fluctuations to render a GB mobile is more accessible for smaller MD supercells.

Curved GBs can be examined using the Kelvin bicrystal model. Similar to bicrystal supercell for flat GBs, there is a large number of choices by referencing the lattice orientations in Table S2. For example, by choosing two grains, A and F, both of octahedral shape and of the same size, one can generate a continue portion of asymmetric and general GBs encapsulated in the MD supercell (Fig. S6 D). It is found that the fully relaxed GBs now resembles another form of minimal surfaces, the well-known Schwarz P-surface (32). In this case, the GB migration is apparently diffusion limited (44). Nevertheless, no thermal stability beyond $0 . 5 T _ { m }$ seems achievable, as long as the initial Kelvin cell is no larger than $6 . 5 \mathrm { n m }$ in size.

The GB energy is evaluated to be about $7 6 1 \mathrm { m J } / \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 }$ for the symmetric Σ9 (22̅1̅) tilt GB. For the asymmetric one, the energy is about $8 7 6 \mathrm { m J } / \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 }$ .

# 3. Mechanical stability of Schwarz crystal

The Schwarz crystal demonstrates enhanced mechanical stability. As demonstrated in Fig. S7 A, the sample yields at an applied strain close to $3 \%$ . The plastic deformation can be maintained up to more than $2 0 \%$ of the applied strain (Fig. S7 B). Tension tests in X and Y directions or with lower loading rate $( \dot { \varepsilon } { = } 1 0 ^ { 5 } / \mathrm { s } )$ ) were also carried out, similar results were obtained. Twinning always constitutes the incipient plasticity events. The yield stress is best estimated to be 1.8~2 GPa. The elastic constants were also calculated at $\mathrm { T } = 0 \mathrm { K }$ (Table S3), showing that the Schwarz crystal is nearly isotropic. Loading tests were also carried out at different temperatures. In short, the Schwarz crystal exhibits enhanced stability against external forces not only at room temperatures but also at high temperatures close to the melting point.

For comparisons, the tensile loading tests were also carried out for the Kelvin sample. Note that before loading, the sample were relaxed at temperatures no more than 300 K. To evaluate the structural evolution of the GB network, the fractions of GB atoms and CTB atoms are extracted and plotted as a function of applied strain (lower panel, Fig. S7 A). The MD snapshots taken at different stages of loading (Fig. S7 C) showed that the sample yielded below $1 . 5 \%$ of the applied strain at a stress less than 650 MPa. However, significant hardening effect can be observed with further loading. In particular, when the applied strain was increased from $1 3 \%$ to $1 6 \%$ , the tension stress may exceed 2 GPa,

during which the fraction of GBs has dropped down from ${ \sim } 3 0 \%$ to ${ \sim } 1 5 \%$ . As manifested by MD snapshots, this occurs as a direct consequence of minimizing the GB area into the Schwarz D-structure form, a process similar to the structural transformation triggered by heating. Deformation at higher temperatures facilities the transformation and structurally more perfect Schwarz crystals can be obtained.
